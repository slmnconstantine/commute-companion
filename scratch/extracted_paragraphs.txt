Paragraph 42 (Found: ['seng', 'li', 'anagnostopoulos', 'dastani', 'psaraftis', 'shah', 'peng']):
Several studies have focused on improving ride-matching and recommendation mechanisms. Anagnostopoulos (2021) developed a predictive ride-sharing recommendation system that utilized artificial intelligence and mobility behavior analysis to recommend suitable commuting partners. The study demonstrated that personalized recommendations improved ride-sharing efficiency and user experiences. Similarly, Anagnostopoulos and Ramson (2025) proposed a ride-sharing algorithm that incorporated spatial, temporal, and social behavior contexts to improve passenger satisfaction. Their findings showed that integrating behavioral information enhanced ride-matching accuracy and commuter satisfaction. Despite these contributions, the studies primarily concentrated on ride recommendations and matching efficiency while providing limited support for real-time commuter interaction and community collaboration. User satisfaction and preferences have also been extensively examined in ride-sharing research. Dastani, Koosha, Karimi, and Moghaddam (2024) developed a preference-driven matching algorithm that incorporated passenger preferences into ride-sharing decisions. The study found that considering user preferences improved matching quality, system responsiveness, and user satisfaction. Likewise, Psaraftis, Ntalianis, and Mastorakis (2024) introduced the Quality of Experience-Oriented Eco-Friendly Taxi-Ride Sharing Recommendation Framework, which considered safety, eco-friendliness, travel costs, and user ratings when generating recommendations. Although these studies improved passenger experiences, they mainly focused on preference optimization and recommendation quality. They provided limited discussion regarding communication tools that allow users to coordinate trips and exchange real-time transportation information. Researchers have also investigated advanced ride-sharing models to improve matching opportunities and transportation efficiency. Shah, El Affendi, and Qureshi (2020) developed SRide, a multi-hop ride-sharing system that enabled passengers to transfer between multiple drivers to reach their destinations. The study reported significant improvements in matching rates and reductions in travel distances. Similarly, Peng, Wang, Wang, and Yu (2026) designed a stable ride-sharing model with role flexibility that allowed commuters to participate as either drivers or riders. Their findings showed increased matching rates and improved ride-sharing participation. However, these studies primarily emphasized optimization and transportation efficiency while paying less attention to commuter safety, trust-building mechanisms, and social interaction among users.
